How to Fascinate - A review

Date Sat 06 June 2015 Tags startup

I was recently asked to write a personal biography for a new startup venture. Personally I hate doing this kind of thing. Anyway, my collegue suggested that I look at the talks and books by Sally Hogshead. I eventually managed to get a copy of the following:

Hogshead, S. (2010). Fascinate: Your 7 triggers to persuasion and captivation. Harper Collins.

I didn't have much time, so I tried to skim read as much as I could. This turned out to be ok as much of the book contains waffle and anecdotes so its easily compressed. Fundamentally what Sally has come up with is a series of 7 topics/themes that you can use to make interesting stories for yourself or products you are selling. I imagine that these are actually just general topics that might apply to writing any interesting story.

The topics/themes that she claims are fascinating are (with my annotated interpretation):

  • Power - Ability or authority to exert some form of control.
  • Vice - A rebellious spirit or maverick. Trying alternative or undesirable pathways.
  • Mystique - Some strange characteristic or ability or trait that is not well understood or not known.
  • Lust - Things that are desirable or provide sensory returns, possibly immediately.
  • Alert - An alarm or call to action. A warning of danger.
  • Trust - Things that are well known, well used and respected.
  • Prestige - Pedigree or related notions.

The book argues that you should base any kind of pitch around a few of these topics/themes to make it engaging and interesting (without obviously stating them).

Interestingly, Sally is now selling online surveys from her website for about $30 each. One thing that I noted is that she has changed her language. Vice has been renamed Innovation and Lust renamed Passion. I suppose that these names are less negative.

Anyway, I attempted to apply her framework towards making my own biography. Here it is below. I don't know if it servers its purpose, but it certainly is the most entertaining one that I have written!

Statistics was a strange place for a teenage skateboarder to end up, but from a few rounds of Tony Hawk's pro skater on Sony's PlayStation 1 came an obsession with technology. After deciding that kick-flips weren't where his real talent lay, he explored how computers worked through a degree in Electrical Engineering. Yet trading heel-flips for D-Flip-Flops wasn't satisfying enough, computers were too contained to do anything truly useful! One thing lead to another and John ended up doing a PhD in robotics, trying to teach computers how to make better decisions when navigating (sadly John's robots were not riding skate boards!). Robots, powered by computers could now navigate, but John's attention now turned to making them smarter, faster and more useful for people outside of skate parks. He joined the National Centre for Information Communication and Technology Australia, Australia's leading research group for Machine Learning. There he discovered how to harness big data and use a new kind of statistics to solve problems that he could never have dreamed of while terrorizing the streets of Wollongong in his youth. John brings a larrikin curiosity, a quest for excellence and, strangely enough, the power of statistics to the North Shore Labs team.
